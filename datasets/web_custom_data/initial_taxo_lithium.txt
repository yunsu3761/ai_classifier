Label: lithium
Dimension: technology_classification
Description: Lithium: Lithium resource extraction and manufacturing is the process of producing lithium compounds from various lithium-bearing sources, including lithium ores (such as spodumene and lepidolite), brines (continental, geothermal, and oilfield brines), and clay-type resources. The primary objective of this process is to convert lithium into compounds such as lithium carbonate (Li₂CO₃), lithium hydroxide (LiOH), lithium chloride (LiCl), and lithium metal to meet the high demand in the electric vehicle and battery industries. Lithium classification encompasses innovations in lithium resource extraction and battery material manufacturing, but it excludes the production of finished lithium-ion batteries or the non-commercial use of lithium resources. Related patents focus on methods for efficiently extracting lithium through various processes such as physical and chemical sorting, evaporation, leaching, and electrochemical separation. For example, these include methods of mixing organic extractants to extract lithium from brine, the production of lithium eluate solutions using ion-exchange reactors, and lithium hydroxide manufacturing processes through multi-stage filtration systems. Additionally, technologies for crushing mixtures and surface modification to manufacture cathode active materials for lithium secondary batteries are included. Such technologies maximize the purity and efficiency of lithium and contribute to improving productivity through customized processes based on resource characteristics.

     Description: Lithium-Upstream: Lithium-Upstream Process refers to the set of extraction and refining operations that process lithium-bearing raw materials, such as hard rock ores (e.g., spodumene, lepidolite) and brines (continental, geothermal, oilfield), into high-purity intermediate products like lithium sulfate (Li₂SO₄) or lithium chloride (LiCl) for subsequent downstream conversion into battery-grade lithium carbonate (Li₂CO₃) or lithium hydroxide (LiOH). Hard rock upstream processes involve mining, crushing, grinding, classification, and beneficiation (flotation or magnetic separation) to produce concentrates, followed by chemical conversion via acid roast or lime roast methods to obtain lithium sulfate solution. Brine upstream processes involve pumping lithium-rich brine, concentrating it through large evaporation ponds or Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) technologies, removing impurities (Na, Ca, Mg, SO₄), and performing carbonation, re-dissolution, or composition conversion steps. The primary goal is to maximize lithium recovery and purity from low-grade ores or brines while minimizing environmental impact and preparing feedstock for downstream lithium salt conversion and crystallization. Patents in this dimension include technologies for ore beneficiation and chemical conversion such as flotation, magnetic separation, acid roasting, lime roasting, and impurity removal from lithium concentrates; brine concentration and purification methods including solar evaporation, chemical precipitation, ion exchange, and advanced DLE systems using selective adsorbents or membranes; process optimizations to improve lithium recovery rates and reduce energy consumption; integrated systems for impurity removal (Na, Ca, Mg, SO₄) and brine composition adjustment; hybrid processes combining evaporation and DLE to overcome climatic and locational constraints; and environmentally friendly upstream methods such as wastewater recycling, low-emission roasting, and by-product recovery from brine or ore processing.

Additionally, innovations targeting energy efficiency in calcination, recovery of lithium from low-grade spodumene, and the recycling of byproducts such as tailings and slag for construction materials are included to enhance economic and environmental sustainability.

               Label: lithium-upstream-ore-lepidolite-processing
               Dimension: technology_classification
               Description: Lithium-Upstream-Ore–Lepidolite Processing: This dimension refers to the upstream processing technologies for lepidolite, a non-traditional hard rock lithium ore belonging to the mica group. Lepidolite has a layered silicate structure and a relatively low lithium content (about 3.0–4.1% Li₂O) compared to spodumene, but is more widely distributed and shares some physical properties with clay minerals. The process generally follows the hard rock upstream sequence—comminution, beneficiation, leaching, and refining—but differs in operating conditions due to its mineral structure. Notably, lepidolite can be leached without calcination, enabling potential energy savings and reduced CO₂ emissions. This dimension focuses on securing lithium sulfate or other lithium-bearing solutions as intermediates for downstream conversion, while addressing challenges such as low ore grade, high tailings and slag generation, and complex impurity removal. Patents in this dimension include beneficiation methods tailored for mica-type ores, such as flotation reagent systems optimized for separating lithium-bearing minerals from quartz and feldspar; low-temperature or calcination-free leaching processes using sulfuric acid or caustic digestion (NaOH) to extract lithium; impurity removal technologies targeting aluminum, potassium, and other elements that complicate refining; and process optimizations to handle high volumes of tailings and slag. Innovations also cover selective impurity precipitation, recovery of byproducts like rubidium and cesium, and valorization of waste materials into construction products. Environmentally friendly approaches, such as CO₂-assisted leaching and energy-efficient reagent usage, are also included to improve the economic and environmental performance of lepidolite-based lithium upstream operations.

               Description: Lithium-Upstream-Ore–Clay/Others: This dimension refers to the extraction and refining technologies for lithium from clay-type minerals (e.g., hectorite, illite) and other non-traditional ore resources. While these resources typically have very low lithium content (0.2–0.4%), their sedimentary nature allows them to be easily processed through water-based disintegration. The core objective is to achieve high recovery rates (over 85%) from low-grade materials through innovative chemical treatments, such as low-temperature roasting with sulfate-based assistants. This technology is strategically important for securing stable lithium supplies as high-grade resources become increasingly scarce. Related patents focus on low-temperature roasting methods (350–750°C) using bisulfates or sulfates as auxiliary agents to enhance lithium solubility without using strong acids, thereby reducing equipment corrosion. For example, these include water leaching processes following selective roasting, methods for managing and stabilizing large volumes of tailings generated from low-grade ores, and technologies for the high-value recycling of by-products. Additionally, innovations in beneficiation that leverage the physical properties of clay minerals—such as their ability to crumble easily in water—to minimize energy-intensive grinding are key. These technologies aim to improve the economic viability of non-traditional resources through process optimization and reduced chemical consumption.

               Description: Lithium-Downstream–Lithium Carbonate:** This dimension refers to downstream refining and conversion technologies that transform lithium intermediates (e.g., lithium sulfate solution, lithium chloride solution, technical-grade lithium carbonate) into high-purity battery-grade lithium carbonate (Li₂CO₃). The process focuses on removing impurities such as Mg, Ca, Na, K, B, and Fe, and controlling particle size, crystal morphology, and purity to meet stringent cathode material specifications. Typical operations include carbonation, purification, and crystallization, with precipitation using sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃) being the most common method. Advanced process control—such as CO₂ gas injection, optimized agitation, temperature, and concentration—aims to improve precipitation efficiency, reduce scaling, and simplify purification steps. This dimension excludes upstream lithium extraction and focuses solely on the final conversion to battery-grade carbonate. Patents in this dimension include precipitation-based lithium carbonate production methods from lithium sulfate or lithium chloride solutions, multi-stage re-dissolution and re-precipitation for impurity removal, and crystallization techniques to control particle size and morphology. Innovations also cover auxiliary purification technologies such as solvent extraction, ion exchange, and bipolar electrodialysis (BPED) to achieve >99.5% purity. Process improvements target scaling prevention, continuous operation, and cost reduction, as well as resource recovery from residues and deposits generated during production. Examples include CO₂-assisted carbonation to enhance reaction control, integrated filtration-washing systems to minimize impurity carryover, and closed-loop recycling of process water and reagents to improve environmental performance.

     Description: Lithium-Downstream: Lithium-Downstream refers to the final conversion, purification, and crystallization stage that transforms upstream-derived lithium intermediates such as lithium sulfate (Li₂SO₄), lithium phosphate (Li₃PO₄), lithium chloride (LiCl), or metallic lithium into high-purity end products, primarily battery-grade lithium carbonate (Li₂CO₃) and lithium hydroxide (LiOH). This dimension focuses on chemical conversion reactions including carbonation and causticization, advanced purification to reduce impurity levels to ppm-grade requirements, and controlled crystallization to meet stringent battery material specifications. Lithium-downstream processes play a decisive role in determining product quality, production cost, and suitability for battery, ceramic, lubricant, and specialty chemical applications, while excluding upstream extraction and lithium-ion battery cell manufacturing. Patents in this dimension include technologies for lithium salt conversion such as carbonation of lithium sulfate or chloride, causticization using Na₂CO₃ or Ca(OH)₂, and direct lithium hydroxide production via bipolar electrodialysis (BPED); high-efficiency purification methods including multi-stage filtration, washing, ion exchange, membrane separation, and electrochemical purification to remove Na, K, Mg, Ca, Fe, and other trace impurities; crystallization and particle size control technologies for producing battery-grade lithium carbonate or lithium hydroxide with consistent morphology; process innovations that minimize auxiliary reagent use, recycle by-products such as Na₂SO₄ and CaCO₃, and recover acids or phosphates within the process loop; and low-energy, low-waste downstream systems designed to improve yield, reduce environmental burden, and enhance overall economic competitiveness of lithium refining operations.
